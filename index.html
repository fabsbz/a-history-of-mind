Three ways to move through the history of mind

Positions & models

A map of the major answers to the question what mind is: soul, form, matter, representation, function, embodiment, social agency, and consciousness-first process.

Block 01

Mind as soul

Mind as a principle of life, inwardness, or spiritual reality rather than merely a mechanism.

  • Thinkers: Plato, Plotinus, Augustine, Upaniṣads, Teresa of Ávila
Block 02

Mind as form of the living body

Hylomorphism, faculties, and the claim that soul names the organizing actuality of embodied life.

  • Thinkers: Aristotle, Alexander of Aphrodisias, Avicenna, Thomas Aquinas, Albert the Great
Block 03

Mind as matter in motion

Materialist and proto-physicalist attempts to explain life and thought without immaterial substance.

  • Thinkers: Lucretius, The Stoics, Thomas Hobbes, La Mettrie, Place & Smart
Block 04

Mind as intellect and illumination

Rational ascent, illumination, and intellect as a privileged route to reality and self-knowledge.

  • Thinkers: Plotinus, al-Fārābī, Avicenna, Averroes, Maimonides, Suhrawardī
Block 05

Mind as self, stream, or bundle

Competing pictures of personal identity, inward unity, and whether the self is stable, shifting, or illusory.

  • Thinkers: Augustine, Hume, William James, Nāgārjuna, Vasubandhu
Block 06

Mind as representation

Ideas, content, intentionality, and inner structure as the basic grammar of mentality.

  • Thinkers: Descartes, Locke, Brentano, Twardowski, Fodor, Rosenthal
Block 07

Mind as function or computation

Mental states defined by role, organization, or information-processing rather than by a special substance.

  • Thinkers: Turing, Putnam, Fodor, Lewis, Baars, Stan Franklin
Block 08

Mind as lived embodiment

Mind understood through body, world, action, and situated experience rather than a detached inner theater.

  • Thinkers: Merleau-Ponty, Heidegger, Uexküll, Varela, Thompson & Rosch, Clark, Colombetti
Block 09

Mind as social or distributed agency

Shared intentionality, collective subjects, and minds scaffolded across persons and institutions.

  • Thinkers: Mead, Durkheim, Tomasello, Gilbert, Tuomela, Pettit, List, Hutchins
Block 10

Mind as integrated or consciousness-first process

Consciousness treated as emergent, integrated, or fundamental enough to resist flat reduction.

  • Thinkers: Fechner, James, Russell, Whitehead, Nagel, Chalmers, Tononi

Methods

A map of the major ways people have tried to know the mind: dialectic, contemplation, phenomenology, experiment, computation, neuroscience, and ethical reflection.

Block 01

Dialectic and metaphysical argument

Using first principles, paradoxes, and conceptual pressure to infer the structure of mind.

  • Thinkers: Plato, Plotinus, Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz
Block 02

Contemplative and spiritual introspection

Disciplined inward attention, meditation, and self-examination as methods of access to consciousness and self.

  • Thinkers: Upaniṣads, Patañjali, Augustine, Teresa of Ávila, John of the Cross, Dōgen
Block 03

Logical and epistemic analysis

Cognition approached through validity, inference, error, and the conditions of successful knowledge.

  • Thinkers: Nyāya, Gautama, Dignāga, Dharmakīrti, Frege, Bolzano
Block 04

Faculty psychology and taxonomic analysis

Mind broken down into powers such as sensation, imagination, memory, intellect, and will.

  • Thinkers: Aristotle, Avicenna, Aquinas, Reid, Herbart
Block 05

Phenomenological description

Careful first-person description of lived experience without reducing it too quickly to mechanism or language.

  • Thinkers: Brentano, Husserl, Heidegger, Sartre, Merleau-Ponty, Scheler
Block 06

Experimental psychology and psychophysics

Measurement, threshold, report, and controlled observation turn mind into a laboratory problem.

  • Thinkers: Fechner, Bain, Ribot, Janet, William James, Ladd-Franklin
Block 07

Evolutionary and comparative inquiry

Mind studied through continuity with animal life, adaptation, behavior, and social intelligence.

  • Thinkers: Darwin, Romanes, Morgan, Lorenz, Tinbergen, de Waal, Tomasello
Block 08

Psychoanalytic and developmental interpretation

Conflict, symbol, attachment, development, and the unconscious as routes to what the mind is doing.

  • Thinkers: Freud, Klein, Bion, Winnicott, Piaget
Block 09

Linguistic and conceptual analysis

Ordinary language and conceptual grammar used to expose confusions about inner episodes and mentality.

  • Thinkers: Wittgenstein, Ryle, Sellars, Cavell, Strawson
Block 10

Computational and formal modeling

Thought investigated by formalization, system design, and information-processing architectures.

  • Thinkers: Turing, Shannon, McCulloch, Simon/Newell, Minsky/Papert, Ashby
Block 11

Neuroscientific and systems approaches

Brain dynamics, global access, prediction, affective systems, and integration as scientific routes to mind.

  • Thinkers: Baars, Dehaene, Tononi, Lamme, Damasio, Seth, LeDoux, Friston/Hohwy/Clark
Block 12

Synthetic and ethical inquiry

We learn what minds are by trying to build them and by asking which systems deserve moral concern.

  • Thinkers: Aleksander, Haikonen, Shanahan, Schneider, Birch, Andrews, Sebo, Nussbaum

Roads to AI

A narrative route from ancient soul-talk to machine minds, artificial consciousness, and debates over synthetic moral status.

Block 01

The first question: what kind of thing is a mind?

Ancient options before computation: soul, form, and material organization.

  • Thinkers: Plato, Aristotle, The Stoics, Lucretius
Block 02

The inward turn and the mechanization problem

Can an inner subject coexist with a mechanistic picture of nature?

  • Thinkers: Augustine, Descartes, Hobbes, Spinoza, Locke
Block 03

Minds as representations and reasoners

Thought becomes structured, rule-governed, and increasingly thinkable as system.

  • Thinkers: Leibniz, Kant, Frege, Brentano, William James
Block 04

From psychology to formal cognition

Mental process becomes measurable, modellable, and open to scientific abstraction.

  • Thinkers: Fechner, Herbart, William James, Turing, Shannon
Block 05

Can thinking be computed?

The classical computational turn in philosophy, cognitive science, and early AI.

  • Thinkers: Turing, McCulloch, Pitts, Simon/Newell, Minsky/Papert, Fodor, Putnam
Block 06

The critics of disembodied AI

Embodiment, skill, and world-involvement challenge purely symbolic models.

  • Thinkers: Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty, Dreyfus, Brooks, Varela/Thompson/Rosch, Clark
Block 07

Machine consciousness proposals

What would have to be true for an artificial system to be conscious rather than merely capable?

  • Thinkers: Aleksander, Haikonen, Franklin, Shanahan, Reggia, Gamez, Schneider
Block 08

Human consciousness as the benchmark

Competing scientific theories used to test whether machine consciousness is even intelligible.

  • Thinkers: Baars, Dehaene, Tononi, Lamme, Seth, Solms
Block 09

Agency without consciousness?

Separating intelligence, agency, intentional stance, and subjective experience.

  • Thinkers: Dennett, Searle, Pettit, Tuomela, List, Tomasello
Block 10

Social and distributed minds

If cognition is scaffolded across systems, artificial mind may not live in a single box.

  • Thinkers: Mead, Durkheim, Hutchins, Clark, Gilbert, Pettit
Block 11

Animal minds as the missing middle

The road from human to machine passes through comparative cognition and sentience.

  • Thinkers: Darwin, Morgan, Griffin, Lorenz, Tinbergen, de Waal, Birch, Andrews
Block 12

If machines suffer, what follows?

The AI story culminates in moral status, precaution, rights, and uncertainty under deep ignorance.

  • Thinkers: Birch, Andrews, Sebo, Nussbaum, Schwitzgebel, Long, Butlin
